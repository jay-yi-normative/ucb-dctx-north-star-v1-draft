What is the North Star?

A menu of partnership models

Why the North Star exists
60%
of pharma–digital health partnerships fail – the #1 cause is no clear partnership strategy at inception.
smartpatient / pharmaphorum synthesis, 2024
High-profile cautionary tales reinforce the stakes: Pear Therapeutics (bankrupt 2023, despite a Novartis partnership) and Proteus Digital Health (bankrupt 2019, despite FDA approval and Otsuka co-launch) both had validated technology – and neither had a watertight partnership strategy or sustainable commercial model.
The UCB · DCTx North Star exists to close that gap – giving teams a structured, repeatable approach to choosing the right partner, structuring the right terms, and knowing when to scale or stop.

The UCB · DCTx North Star is a practical decision framework that helps UCB teams choose, structure, and sustain partnerships with digital health companies at any maturity stage. It does not create new bureaucracy – it removes the ambiguity that slows partnerships down and replaces ad-hoc decisions with a repeatable, defensible process.

✓ A Partnership

Both sides have skin in the game. Mutual risk. Mutual upside. Shared accountability for outcomes. Both parties bring something the other cannot replicate alone – and both stand to gain or lose from how the partnership performs.

✕ A Vendor Relationship

One party implements a strategy the other defines. Transactional. Fee-for-service. The vendor delivers a deliverable; UCB retains all accountability for outcomes. This framework does not apply to vendor or agency relationships – those are procurement decisions, not partnership decisions.

✓  What it WILL do
Give you a menu of 4 proven collaboration models to choose from based on your startup's maturity and your team's goals
Define clear vision, requirements, value exchange, and capabilities for each model
Show you which third-party partners may be needed to close capability gaps
Provide real case studies – one from UCB and two from top pharma competitors – for each model
Help commercial, brand, finance, and legal teams align before a partnership conversation begins
Give you the language to write a fair-value-exchange contract that gets approved
Provide a legal on-ramp – each model includes pre-scoped compliance considerations so legal is a partner in the process from Day 1, not a final gate that defaults to "no"
✕  What it will NOT do
Replace existing UCB compliance, legal, or stage gate processes
Make the decision for you – it guides, you decide
Treat all partnerships the same regardless of TA, maturity, or strategic fit
Add another framework that sits in a drawer – it's built to be used in live conversations
Be static – it evolves with each version and activation workshop
✕  What UCB is NOT looking for in a partner approach
–

"We need your money." Capital is not the primary thing UCB offers. Partners who lead with a funding ask before demonstrating a clear problem-solution fit and strategic alignment will not get traction. UCB offers market access, clinical credibility, and evidence infrastructure – not a grant.

–

"Here's our solution – what are you working on?" Partners who arrive with a finished product and expect UCB to find a use case for it. The right approach is the reverse: understand UCB's care transformation priorities, then show how your technology advances them.

–

Generic digital wellness or professional services. This framework is not for consulting firms, marketing agencies, or app vendors delivering general wellness content. Partnerships must involve a digital health company with a product driving measurable care outcomes.

–

Pilots with no exit plan. Partners who cannot define what "success" looks like at 3, 6, or 12 months – or who assume UCB will keep funding indefinitely without measurable outcomes – are not partnership-ready. The North Star requires a pre-agreed scale/continue/stop framework before any pilot begins.

Legal & Risk Tolerance – Start Here

UCB's legal and compliance teams default to "no" when approached without context. The question to ask is not "can we do this?" but "how do we structure this so we can?" – and that conversation starts here.

Before you choose a model

Build, Partner, or Buy?

Before selecting a partnership model, UCB teams must first answer a prior question: should we build this capability in-house, partner for it, or acquire/invest in it? Defaulting to a partnership when the right answer is "build" leads to IP loss, dependency, and capability gaps.

"We are a drug company. We are not a tech company. We have pretended to be a tech company multiple times – and our internal capabilities just are not designed to support technology as its own intervention."
— Kim Moran, Strategic Partnerships, UCB

🔧 Build In-House

When the capability is core to UCB's long-term competitive advantage and requires deep integration with drug development, HEOR, or medical affairs processes.

Examples: patient journey analytics, internal HEOR modeling, clinical trial design capabilities, HCP engagement infrastructure.
🤝 Partner

When the capability requires speed, specialist expertise, or patient-facing technology that UCB cannot build at pace – but where UCB contributes complementary value (clinical networks, payer access, data infrastructure).

Examples: digital biomarker platforms, care navigation apps, DTx products, RWE data capture tools. → Use one of the 4 North Star models.
💰 Buy / Invest

When the capability is strategically critical but time-to-build is prohibitive – and the market has a validated company that UCB can acquire or take a strategic stake in, with defined access rights.

Examples: first-mover disease monitoring platform in a priority therapeutic area, rare disease patient data lake. → Use Model 4 (Equity / Investment).

Rule of thumb: if UCB would not want to own or maintain the technology in 5 years, do not build it. If the capability requires a commercial partner's patient relationships or market access to deliver value, partner – don't replicate. If neither applies, it may be a vendor engagement, not a partnership.

How to use this framework

Cross-functional engagement – in the right order

Multiple UCB leaders said "bring us in early" – but without a sequence, that creates paralysis. This is the recommended engagement order before any external partner conversation begins.

1
DCTx / Strategy

Define the model fit and care transformation hypothesis. Confirm this is a partnership (not a vendor) and it maps to a North Star model.

2
Legal & Compliance

Review the model's pre-scoped must-align requirements. Confirm data governance scope, IP boundaries, and risk window before any partner is approached.

3
Finance

Validate the fair value exchange structure. Confirm green/brown money framing and identify budget line before any partner receives a term sheet or LOI.

4
Brand / Commercial

Commit to shared KPIs and a brand investment narrative. Define how partner outcomes map to brand goals before the commercial team is engaged.

5
External Partner

Now engage the partner with a clear model, defined terms, confirmed budget, and pre-agreed KPIs. Not before.

Engage a payer advisory firm at study design stage, not after data collection.

⚖
Pilot Governance – Scale / Continue / Stop

Agree on these decision gates before data collection begins. If the gate criteria are not met, the default decision is Stop – not "extend three more months."

✓ Scale
  • Evidence endpoints met at pre-agreed statistical threshold
  • Payer review confirms dossier is submission-ready
  • Partner has demonstrated IRB/data compliance at scale
  • Joint publication or dissemination plan confirmed
⟳ Continue
  • Evidence trending positive but sample size not yet sufficient
  • One material gap remains (e.g., specific payer market not yet covered)
  • Continuation requires a defined new milestone – not open-ended
✕ Stop
  • Evidence endpoints not met after agreed study period
  • Data governance or IP disputes unresolved after 60 days
  • Payer explicitly confirms evidence is insufficient for submission
  • Partner has not fulfilled co-design or publication commitments
REAL EXAMPLES – 3 CASE STUDIES
UCB × Beacon Biosignals
Epilepsy EEG RWE – Co-designing real-world evidence on seizure patterns
UCB EXAMPLE US / EU

Partnership Vision

UCB engaged Beacon Biosignals to explore how ambulatory EEG data from its Dreem headband could generate real-world evidence on seizure patterns and treatment response – supporting clinical validation of remote monitoring and reimbursement pathways for UCB's epilepsy portfolio.

What UCB Brought

Epilepsy clinical network for patient recruitment, co-design of evidence framework with medical affairs, HEOR modeling to translate clinical findings into payer-facing economic arguments, and connections to payer and regulatory contacts that Beacon could not access alone.

What Beacon Brought

Proprietary ambulatory EEG technology (Dreem headband), longitudinal EEG data from 800+ patients not available anywhere else, digital biomarker capabilities for passive seizure detection, and a clinical science team.

Key Learning

Evidence design must align early with payer evidence thresholds – not post-data-collection. A shared data governance agreement must be the critical first step. Seizure monitoring accuracy was questioned at Digital Vibes workshop (May 2026) – validation evidence must be established before UCB co-invests at scale.

⚠ Partnership status update: Beacon Biosignals was acquired by Medtronic in 2024. UCB should confirm the current partnership terms and data rights are still operative under Medtronic ownership before any new co-investment or evidence study is initiated.

Commercial team distraction risk:
"Do not involve the field team early unless we plan to do something with them and there's alignment – you put this in any field team and they're talking, it's going to distract them."

Without two separate governance tracks – one for regulatory/compliance submissions, one for product iteration – a co-launch partnership will paralyze the partner's product development while UCB's compliance processes catch up. UCB × Clouds of Care
Epilepsy Care Navigation – Facilitated HCP adoption via UCB's field infrastructure
UCB EXAMPLE US

What They Explored

UCB explored a commercial G2M collaboration with Clouds of Care, a digital platform connecting epilepsy patients to care resources, navigating insurance barriers, and supporting medication adherence. UCB's field force and TLLs were positioned as facilitators to introduce the platform to epilepsy centers of excellence.

UCB's Role

UCB's commercial team provides access to HCP relationships and the epilepsy center network. TLLs facilitate introductions and build platform awareness above the product sales conversation. UCB's brand association gives credibility with providers skeptical of unproven digital tools.

Key Learning

Agreeing on what constitutes "value" for UCB (adherence data? brand lift? prescription uplift?) BEFORE engaging is essential. Without an aligned commercial model, pharma teams default to ad-hoc support and never commit sufficient resources. A pilot with defined success metrics – tied to Vimpat or Fintepla adherence data – is the recommended first step.

Commercial G2M Warning Cases
Otsuka × Proteus and Pear Therapeutics × Novartis – when validated technology meets an unresolved business model
US

Otsuka × Proteus – Abilify MyCite (FDA-approved 2017, Proteus bankrupt 2019)

Otsuka partnered with Proteus Digital Health to embed an FDA-cleared ingestible sensor into Abilify (aripiprazole), creating Abilify MyCite – the first FDA-approved digital medicine. The technology was validated. The co-launch failed because payer alignment was never secured before commercial activation. Proteus entered bankruptcy in 2019.

Pear Therapeutics × Novartis – reSET DTx (Pear bankrupt 2023)

Pear Therapeutics developed FDA-cleared prescription digital therapeutics (PDTs) for substance use disorder (reSET) and insomnia (Somryst), with Novartis as a strategic partner and investor. Despite FDA clearance, a major pharma backer, and a first-mover position in prescription DTx, Pear filed for bankruptcy in January 2023. The cause: no sustainable payer reimbursement model. Most insurers refused to cover prescription DTx as a standalone benefit. Novartis's commercial infrastructure was not sufficient to substitute for a missing reimbursement strategy.

The shared lesson for UCB

Both cases confirm the same finding: technology validation and even FDA/CE clearance are insufficient for commercial success without a funded reimbursement pathway agreed before launch. For UCB's Commercial G2M partnerships: the payer strategy is a prerequisite to field activation – not a post-launch problem. If the partner cannot articulate a credible coverage pathway at model selection stage, this is not a Commercial G2M partner yet – consider Model 1 (RWE evidence co-generation) as the correct first step.

UCB – Strategic Digital Health Investment Approach
Structuring equity investments for strategic return, not just financial exposure
UCB EXAMPLE US / EU

The UCB Context

UCB has made strategic investments in digital health companies where the technology offers direct complementary value to its therapeutic portfolio – including Byteflies (10-year partner, epilepsy EEG monitoring) and Beacon Biosignals. The investment model has provided UCB with preferred access and a stake in the partner's commercial success.

The North Star Contract Checklist

Whichever model you choose, every UCB digital health partnership contract should address these elements to enable budget approval, sustainability, and a strong internal narrative:

FAIR VALUE EXCHANGE
What does UCB get? What does the partner get? Measured by what KPIs? Green money (revenue) or brown money (cost savings) narrative clearly stated.
DATA & IP RIGHTS
Who owns the data generated? Who owns jointly developed IP? What are the usage rights? These must be resolved before data collection begins.
SUSTAINABILITY MODEL
Who pays for what, and for how long? What is the exit or renewal criteria? A Care Compass-style "who pays next year?" crisis is prevented by having this defined at contract signature.
