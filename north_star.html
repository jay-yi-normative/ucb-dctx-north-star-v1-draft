UCB · Digital Health & Care Transformation Partnership Framework

UCB · DCTx
North Star

A decision framework for UCB teams identifying, evaluating, and structuring partnerships with digital health companies committed to transforming care – for patients, clinicians, and health systems. Built exclusively for partnerships that drive digital and care transformation. Not for vendors, agencies, or professional services engagements.

High-profile cautionary tales reinforce the stakes: Pear Therapeutics (bankrupt 2023, despite a Novartis partnership) and Proteus Digital Health (bankrupt 2019, despite FDA approval and Otsuka co-launch) both had validated technology – and neither had a watertight partnership strategy or sustainable commercial model.
The UCB · DCTx North Star exists to close that gap – giving teams a structured, repeatable approach to choosing the right partner, structuring the right terms, and knowing when to scale or stop.

Step 1 · Filter

Is this the right kind of partner?

✓ Green Flags
What UCB IS looking for in a partner
✓

A real, documented patient unmet need — not a solution looking for a problem. Partners who start from "what does this patient population actually need" earn UCB's attention. Partners who reverse-engineer a use case onto their existing product are a consistent source of failed pilots.

✓

Past the pilot stage with a go-to-market model they can actually execute. UCB cannot be a distribution channel for an unproven product. Partners must demonstrate they can reach patients or HCPs at scale — not just in a controlled study setting.

✓

A clear, measurable outcome — revenue generated or costs saved — within a reasonable time horizon. Without a direct or credibly indirect financial story, the partnership will stall at the UCB investment committee level. Partners who only tell an altruistic or brand-halo story will not survive resource allocation decisions against drug assets.

✓

A differentiated capability that UCB cannot build internally at the same speed or quality. Whether it's a physician network, a telehealth platform, EHR integrations, or a proprietary clinical algorithm — the partner must own something real that UCB genuinely needs.

✓

Willingness to share de-identified data back to UCB in a compliant format — with the infrastructure to actually do so. UCB's ability to generate real-world evidence and justify continued investment depends on data coming in house. Partners who retain all data leave UCB with nothing when the contract ends.

✓

Ability to operate in the relevant geography without being entirely dependent on UCB to scale. If UCB is the partner's only path to market, the dynamic becomes unsustainable. UCB should be a co-creator of value — not a company's entire distribution strategy.

✓

Already understands UCB's therapeutic areas — without UCB having to teach them the patient journey. UCB's areas (epilepsy, HS, rare syndromes, immunology) are underserved and complex. Partners who arrive with generic platforms and expect UCB to explain the disease state drain internal resources. The right partner already knows the care gap.

✕ Red Flags
What UCB is NOT looking for in a partner
✕

"We need your money." Capital is not the primary thing UCB offers. Partners who lead with a funding ask before demonstrating a clear problem-solution fit and strategic alignment will not get traction. UCB offers market access, clinical credibility, and evidence infrastructure – not a grant.

✕

"Here's our solution – what are you working on?" Partners who arrive with a finished product and expect UCB to find a use case for it. The right approach is the reverse: understand UCB's care transformation priorities, then show how your technology advances them.

✕

Generic digital wellness or professional services. This framework is not for consulting firms, marketing agencies, or app vendors delivering general wellness content. Partnerships must involve a digital health company with a product driving measurable care outcomes.

✕

Pilots with no exit plan. Partners who cannot define what "success" looks like at 3, 6, or 12 months – or who assume UCB will keep funding indefinitely without measurable outcomes – are not partnership-ready. The North Star requires a pre-agreed scale/continue/stop framework before any pilot begins.

Step 2 · Evaluate

Know where your partner is before choosing a model

Digital health startups exist across a spectrum of maturity. The partnership model that works for a Series B commercial-stage company will fail with a pre-revenue prototype. The North Star maps each of its 4 models to the maturity stages where they create the most value – and flags where the risks are highest.

UCB · Active
UCB × Beacon Biosignals
Epilepsy EEG RWE – Co-designing real-world evidence on seizure patterns
▼

Partnership Vision

UCB engaged Beacon Biosignals to explore how ambulatory EEG data from its Dreem headband could generate real-world evidence on seizure patterns and treatment response – supporting clinical validation of remote monitoring and reimbursement pathways for UCB's epilepsy portfolio.

What UCB Brought

Epilepsy clinical network for patient recruitment, co-design of evidence framework with medical affairs, HEOR modeling to translate clinical findings into payer-facing economic arguments, and connections to payer and regulatory contacts that Beacon could not access alone.

What Beacon Brought

Proprietary ambulatory EEG technology (Dreem headband), longitudinal EEG data from 800+ patients not available anywhere else, digital biomarker capabilities for passive seizure detection, and a clinical science team.

Key Learning

Evidence design must align early with payer evidence thresholds – not post-data-collection. A shared data governance agreement must be the critical first step. Seizure monitoring accuracy was questioned at Digital Vibes workshop (May 2026) – validation evidence must be established before UCB co-invests at scale.

⚠ Partnership status update: Beacon Biosignals was acquired by Medtronic in 2024. UCB should confirm the current partnership terms and data rights are still operative under Medtronic ownership before any new co-investment or evidence study is initiated.

Source: UCB 3rd Epilepsy Digital Roundtable (Dec 2025); Beacon Biosignals company disclosures; Digital Vibes Workshop Summary (May 2026)

▼

Partnership Vision

AZ partnered with Huma (formerly Medopad) to deploy a digital monitoring platform for heart failure patients across NHS sites, collecting real-time patient-reported outcomes and medication adherence data to build an RWE dossier for NICE submissions and EU market access.

Novartis – MS-PATHS RWE Consortium
Multi-pharma RWE network with digital health and academic medical centers
▼

Partnership Vision

Multiple pharma companies including Novartis co-funded the MS-PATHS network – a collaboration with academic medical centers and Flatiron Health to collect standardized real-world data on MS disease progression and treatment outcomes across 10,000+ patients.

Cautionary
Commercial G2M Warning Cases
Otsuka × Proteus and Pear Therapeutics × Novartis – when validated technology meets an unresolved business model
▼

Otsuka × Proteus – Abilify MyCite (FDA-approved 2017, Proteus bankrupt 2019)

Otsuka partnered with Proteus Digital Health to embed an FDA-cleared ingestible sensor into Abilify (aripiprazole), creating Abilify MyCite – the first FDA-approved digital medicine. The technology was validated. The co-launch failed because payer alignment was never secured before commercial activation. Proteus entered bankruptcy in 2019.

Pear Therapeutics × Novartis – reSET DTx (Pear bankrupt 2023)

Pear Therapeutics developed FDA-cleared prescription digital therapeutics (PDTs) for substance use disorder (reSET) and insomnia (Somryst), with Novartis as a strategic partner and investor. Despite FDA clearance, a major pharma backer, and a first-mover position in prescription DTx, Pear filed for bankruptcy in January 2023. The cause: no sustainable payer reimbursement model. Most insurers refused to cover prescription DTx as a standalone benefit. Novartis's commercial infrastructure was not sufficient to substitute for a missing reimbursement strategy.

The shared lesson for UCB

Both cases confirm the same finding: technology validation and even FDA/CE clearance are insufficient for commercial success without a funded reimbursement pathway agreed before launch. For UCB's Commercial G2M partnerships: the payer strategy is a prerequisite to field activation – not a post-launch problem. If the partner cannot articulate a credible coverage pathway at model selection stage, this is not a Commercial G2M partner yet – consider Model 1 (RWE evidence co-generation) as the correct first step.

UCB – Strategic Digital Health Investment Approach
Structuring equity investments for strategic return, not just financial exposure
▼

The UCB Context

UCB has made strategic investments in digital health companies where the technology offers direct complementary value to its therapeutic portfolio – including Byteflies (10-year partner, epilepsy EEG monitoring) and Beacon Biosignals. The investment model has provided UCB with preferred access and a stake in the partner's commercial success.

The Shift Required

Based on leadership interviews (Jason Joseph, Erik Janssen) and Tanja Kellermann's direction, UCB is moving away from pure equity investments toward operational MoRAL P-type partnerships. Future equity investments must include structured strategic access terms: data rights for RWE, preferred HCP access rights, or first-mover commercial partnerships – not just a stake on the cap table.

Key Learning

Pharma equity investments that lack clear strategic terms consistently underperform. The companies generating the most value (Novartis, Sanofi, Roche) all tie equity to structured strategic access agreements. UCB's next investment should include: explicit data rights clause, preferred commercial terms for epilepsy, and board observer rights.

Source: UCB leadership interviews (Jason Joseph, Apr 2026; Erik Janssen, Apr 2026); Normative project files; McKinsey Digital Health Investment Report (2023); Rock Health (2024)

Peer Case
Novartis Venture Fund
Corporate venture fund with structured strategic alignment to therapeutic priorities
▼

What Novartis Did

Novartis established its Venture Fund (NVF) to invest in early-stage digital health and biotech companies whose technology complements Novartis's clinical and commercial priorities – including AI diagnostics, digital therapeutics, and patient monitoring – all structured with strategic access rights, not just financial stakes. Each NVF investment includes: data rights for Novartis's medical affairs team, right of first negotiation for commercial partnerships, and board observer rights.

UCB Lesson

Portfolio investment (5–10 companies at different stages) generates more strategic options than single large bets. UCB should consider a staged portfolio approach across UCB's therapeutic priorities rather than concentrated single investments. A small UCB Digital Health Fund could hold preferred access rights across strategic startups in disease monitoring, diagnostics, and care navigation across UCB's therapeutic priorities.

What They Did

Alphabet's GV and Verily take equity stakes in digital health companies that generate data or clinical capabilities feeding into Alphabet's broader digital health infrastructure – including partnerships with Novartis, Sanofi, and Pfizer structured with preferred data access and commercial integration rights.

UCB Lesson

Invest in platform companies where the strategic return is data access and clinical integration rights, not just financial return. UCB can use equity investment to secure rights to digital biomarker data from epilepsy monitoring platforms – a strategic asset for RWE and commercial goals. Verily's partnerships with pharma generated shared RWE datasets used in regulatory submissions and payer negotiations: preferred data access is commercially and clinically strategic, not speculative.
